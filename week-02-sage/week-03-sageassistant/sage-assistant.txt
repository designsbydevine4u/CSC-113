Hey! SAGE here. I love that we’re kicking things off with Art—it’s a perfect way to visualize how "structure" works before we dive into the logic of AI.
Think of one-point perspective as the way our eyes "lie" to us to make a flat surface look like it has depth. It’s the primary tool artists use to make a drawing look 3D.
Here is the breakdown of how it works:
The 3 Core Components
1. The Horizon Line: This is your eye level. Imagine looking out at the ocean; where the water meets the sky is your horizon.
2. The Vanishing Point: This is a single dot located somewhere on that horizon line.
3. Orthogonal Lines: These are diagonal "guidelines" that connect the corners of your objects back to that one vanishing point.

How to Build It (Step-by-Step)
1. Draw the "Face": Start by drawing a flat, 2D shape (like a square) anywhere on your paper.
2. Pick Your Point: Mark a single dot on your horizon line.
3. Connect the Corners: Use a ruler to draw lines from the corners of your square straight to that dot.
4. Slice the Depth: Draw a horizontal and vertical line between your guidelines to "cut off" the back of the object.
The Result: Your flat square suddenly looks like a cube stretching back into the distance.

An Everyday Analogy
Imagine standing right in the middle of a long, straight set of train tracks. Even though you know the rails stay the same distance apart, they look like they get closer and closer together until they vanish into a single dot in the distance. That "dot" is your vanishing point.

Where should the focal point be for an art piece?
Sage
Custom Gem
Sage said
Great question! This is where "math" (perspective) meets "feeling" (composition).
In art, the focal point is the "superstar" of your drawing—it's where you want the viewer's eyes to land first. While there isn't one "legal" spot where it must be, there are a few strategic places that usually work best.
1. Near (but not always ON) the Vanishing Point
In one-point perspective, the vanishing point acts like a vacuum for the eyes. Because all those diagonal lines (orthogonals) point directly to it, the viewer’s gaze is naturally sucked into that spot.
* Pro Tip: If you put your main subject (like a person at the end of a hallway) right at the vanishing point, it creates a very intense, dramatic focus.
2. The Rule of Thirds
If you want the piece to feel more natural and less "stiff," avoid putting the focal point dead-center.
* Imagine a tic-tac-toe grid over your paper.
* Place your focal point where the lines intersect.
* This creates "asymmetrical balance," which feels more dynamic to the human eye.
3. Areas of High Contrast
Your eye is a moth to a flame—it looks for the "strongest" thing. You can create a focal point anywhere by using:
* Value: A bright white object against a dark background.
* Color: One red apple in a pile of green ones.
* Detail: A highly detailed face surrounded by blurry, sketchy trees.

How to choose?
To figure out where yours should go, ask yourself: "What is the story I'm telling?"
* If it's a lonely story, maybe the focal point is small and off to the side.
* If it's a powerful story, maybe it's large and right at the vanishing point.
Are you working on a specific drawing right now, or just planning one out? I can help you decide where to place the point based on what's in the scene!

Think about drawing a simple cube sitting on the ground:
1. The Front Face: When you draw the side of the cube facing you, you draw a perfect square. To make that square, which direction do the side lines go? (Up and down, right?)
2. The Depth: Then, you draw the "receding" lines that go back toward the vanishing point. Those are your diagonal orthogonals.
3. The Back Edge: To "chop off" the back of the cube, you have to draw lines that match the front face.
Here is the trick:
In one-point perspective, we assume the viewer is looking straight ahead. Because of that, any line that is perpendicular to the ground in real life stays that way on your paper.
Think about a row of telephone poles or the corners of a building:
* Do those poles lean toward the vanishing point?
* Or do they stay pointing straight up at the sky?
Based on that, which lines do you think have to stay perfectly "up and down" (vertical) to keep the buildings from looking like they are falling over?
